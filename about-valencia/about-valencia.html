Valencia, Negros Oriental rises gracefully in the highlands, just 9 kilometers (5.6 miles) west of Dumaguete City, offering a refreshing contrast to the coastal bustle below. With a population of over 31,000 inhabitants, this municipality has earned admiration for its pristine environment, proudly acclaimed in 2007 as the “Greenest and Cleanest Town” of the province. Its cultural soul shines each October 12, when the community gathers in vibrant devotion to honor their patron saint, Our Lady of the Abandoned, through a spirited town fiesta rich with tradition, music, and festivity. Comprising 24 barangays, Valencia is a mosaic of heritage and progress, where the Cebuano dialect flows naturally yet English remains widely spoken, ensuring visitors feel both welcomed and understood. With its cool climate, verdant scenery, and warm hospitality, Valencia truly embodies the charm of “The Little Baguio of Negros.”

Brief History

The municipality was once called Ermita, meaning “a secluded place,” as it served as a refuge from the attacks of marauding Muslim pirates. In 1856, Spanish colonizers renamed it Nueva Valencia in honor of its parish priest who hailed from Valencia in Spain. Later, in 1920, the town was given the name Luzuriaga, paying tribute to Don Carlos Luzuriaga, a delegate from Negros Island to the Philippine Legislature. Finally, in 1948, the municipality adopted the name it proudly carries today—Valencia.

Guide to Valencia, Negros Oriental

Known as “The Little Baguio of Negros”, Valencia lies just 9 kilometers from the bustling streets of Dumaguete City and is best reached by hopping on a local jeepney, with the terminal conveniently located south of the Public Market. Jeepneys depart every 15 minutes, making the short 15–20 minute journey both easy and affordable at only Php 15.00. While private tricycles can be arranged for a more exclusive ride, the jeepney remains the most practical choice—not only for its budget-friendly fare but also for the authentic cultural experience it offers, allowing travelers to immerse themselves in the everyday rhythm of Filipino life.

Economy of Valencia

Valencia is a vital producer of electricity through its natural geothermal resources, with a power station operated by the Energy Development Corporation in Barangay Puhagan that supplies energy not only to Negros but also to Panay and parts of Cebu. Alongside this, the municipality’s economy thrives on agriculture, most notably its famous lanzones fruits which are exported across the province. Other key products include copra, abaca, corn, vegetables, flowers, root crops, and exotic fruits such as rambutan, making Valencia both an energy hub and a rich agricultural heartland of Negros Oriental.
